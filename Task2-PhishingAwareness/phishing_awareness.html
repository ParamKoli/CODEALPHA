📌 What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of cyber attack where attackers impersonate legitimate organizations or individuals to trick victims into revealing sensitive information such as passwords, credit card numbers, or personal data.

Phishing attacks are responsible for over 90% of data breaches worldwide, making it the most common and dangerous form of cybercrime today.

🎯 Types of Phishing Attacks

📧 Email Phishing

The most common form — attackers send mass emails pretending to be banks, government agencies, or popular services like PayPal or Amazon, tricking users into clicking malicious links.

🎯 Spear Phishing

Highly targeted attacks aimed at specific individuals or organizations. Attackers research their targets beforehand to craft convincing personalized messages.

🐳 Whaling

Spear phishing specifically targeting high-profile executives (CEOs, CFOs). These attacks often involve fraudulent wire transfers or executive impersonation.

📱 Smishing (SMS Phishing)

Phishing attacks delivered via SMS text messages. Common examples include fake delivery notifications or bank alerts with malicious links.

📞 Vishing (Voice Phishing)

Phone-based phishing where attackers call victims pretending to be tech support, bank officials, or government agents to extract sensitive information.

🌐 Pharming

Attackers redirect users from legitimate websites to fake ones without their knowledge, even when the user types the correct URL.

🔍 How to Recognize Phishing

Learning to spot phishing attempts is your first line of defense. Here are the key warning signs:

  • ⚠️ Urgency and Fear: Messages that create panic — "Your account will be suspended in 24 hours!"
  • ⚠️ Suspicious Sender Address: Email from "support@paypa1.com" instead of "support@paypal.com"
  • ⚠️ Generic Greetings: "Dear Customer" instead of your actual name
  • ⚠️ Suspicious Links: Hover over links before clicking — the URL shown doesn't match the destination
  • ⚠️ Poor Grammar: Spelling mistakes, awkward phrasing, or unusual formatting
  • ⚠️ Unexpected Attachments: Unsolicited files, especially .exe, .zip, or .docm files
  • ⚠️ Requests for Sensitive Info: Legitimate organizations never ask for passwords via email
  • ⚠️ Too Good to Be True: Prize notifications, lottery wins, or unexpected refunds

📋 Real-World Phishing Examples

🏦 Example 1 — Fake Bank Alert

Subject: "URGENT: Your account has been compromised — Immediate action required"

From: security@bank0famerica.com

Message: "We have detected suspicious activity on your account. Click here immediately to verify your identity and prevent your account from being locked."

🚩 Red Flags:

  • Fake domain — "bank0famerica" uses zero instead of 'o'
  • Creates urgency and fear
  • Asks you to click a link to "verify identity"
  • Real banks never ask for credentials via email

📦 Example 2 — Fake Delivery Notification

SMS: "Your Amazon package could not be delivered. Update your delivery address here: http://amaz0n-delivery.tk/update"

🚩 Red Flags:

  • Suspicious URL — not amazon.com
  • Uses .tk domain (free, commonly used by scammers)
  • Unsolicited SMS about a package you didn't order
  • Asks for personal/payment information

🏢 Example 3 — CEO Fraud (Whaling)

From: ceo.johnsmith@company-corp.net (fake)

To: Finance Department

Message: "Hi, I'm in an urgent meeting and need you to process a wire transfer of $50,000 immediately. I'll explain later. Don't discuss this with anyone."

🚩 Red Flags:

  • Requests secrecy — "don't discuss with anyone"
  • Extreme urgency with no explanation
  • Email domain doesn't match company domain
  • Unusual financial request via email

🧠 Social Engineering Tactics

Phishing attacks exploit human psychology rather than technical vulnerabilities. Here are the key psychological tactics attackers use:

  • 🔴 Authority: Impersonating bosses, banks, government agencies, or law enforcement to command compliance
  • 🔴 Urgency: Creating time pressure so victims act without thinking ("Your account expires in 1 hour!")
  • 🔴 Fear: Threatening consequences to force immediate action ("You have a virus — call now!")
  • 🔴 Curiosity: Luring victims with intriguing content ("See who viewed your profile")
  • 🔴 Greed: Promising rewards, prizes, or refunds to entice action
  • 🔴 Trust: Building rapport before asking for sensitive information
  • 🔴 Reciprocity: Offering something free first to create a sense of obligation

✅ Best Practices to Stay Safe

✔️ Verify Before You Click

Always hover over links to preview the URL before clicking. If in doubt, go directly to the website by typing the address manually.

✔️ Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Even if attackers steal your password, MFA prevents them from accessing your account without the second factor.

✔️ Keep Software Updated

Regular updates patch security vulnerabilities that phishing attacks often exploit to install malware.

✔️ Use Anti-Phishing Tools

Enable browser-based phishing protection, use email spam filters, and consider a password manager that won't autofill on fake sites.

✔️ When in Doubt, Call Directly

If you receive a suspicious email from your bank or employer, call them directly using the official number — never use contact details from the suspicious email.

✔️ Report Phishing Attempts

Report phishing emails to your IT department, email provider, and relevant authorities (e.g., report@phishing.gov.in in India).
